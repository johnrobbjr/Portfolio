Redesigning the Tissue Analytics Landing Page

August, 2016

 Home

We were just starting to get trail customers and this landing page was the gatekeeper between an interested user and a trail user, then hopefully a paying user.

The website was generated from a template, and the content and interactions were not created with the user in mind.
Our current landing page was not responsive, and by looking at our analytics, we were receiving 10% of our traffic through mobile devices. I determined that this was enough reason to warrant a responsive website. UX pitfalls were scattered around the website including dead-end flows and unnecessary redundancies.

Challenges

Time: I had about 3 weeks to complete the redesign, as no designer was scheduled to start once I left to pick up the project.
Team: I was the only designer on the team, so I would use my team to get feedback on my designs and ask essential questions about the page’s users and purpose.
Experience: I had not designed a full web landing page before, so research and competitive analysis would drive some design decisions.

Process

After talking to a number of team members, I found out what users we would target for a free trail, and what information they would be looking for. I did research on competitor’s websites as well as information on designing landing pages with high conversion rates that I followed throughout the design process.
First I completed some low-fidelity sketches for rapid iteration on the concept. Throughout this process I was asking for constant feedback and critiques from team-members to keep everyone involved.

I then moved into medium-fidelity wireframes

After a couple of weeks of iterations on the high-fidelity layer comps, I had a finished landing page for web completed.

As this was my first full web design project, at this point I quickly learned the value of mobile first as I transitioned full-width comps into mobile- and tablet-optimized screens.

The assets were then passed on to our front-end developer, and we kept open communication about what had to be done for proper implementation and clarifications on the assets.
Two weeks later the website was up and running, and is live (with minor changes since I left the company) at tissue-analytics.com

What Went Well

Communicating with the entire startup throughout the process not only lead to a substantial product, but also increased comradery between team members that didn’t typically get to work on mutual projects.
The project unveiled flaws in the users workflow that were able to be solved with design solutions that I implemented in the new design.

What I Would Do Differently

Creating a customer journey map could have illustrated these problems to stakeholders, driving design solutions for the landing page as well as future projects.
Developing personas earlier in with user interviews could have informed the design of the website.
Using a mobile-first strategy, creating a simpler UI optimized for mobile devices then scaling them up would have saved time and effort.
